There's a specific kind of silence that follows disagreeing with someone more senior than you when you're the youngest person in the room and everyone knows it.

I got to know that silence early. The story of the last few years, if I'm honest, is mostly the story of learning it couldn't actually hurt me.

## The second engineer

I was a few months into my first year of college when I joined a fantasy football startup as its second engineer. Small team, nowhere to hide, and I was the youngest person in it, though not by much.

In the early meetings a decision would come up, I'd have an actual opinion about it, and then I'd watch myself swallow the opinion whole. Who was I to push? Everyone else had more road behind them than I did. So I folded. Someone would lean on a point and I'd cave, not because they'd changed my mind but because that silence after you disagree felt unbearable when you're the kid.

What changed wasn't some sudden burst of confidence. It was noticing, days later, how many times I'd given in on things I turned out to be right about. That stung more than losing the argument ever did. So I started staying in them. Holding the point one beat longer. Asking the next question instead of nodding through it.

A team that small doesn't let you stay in your lane, either. I was there to write code, but I ended up in rooms I had no business being in at that age. On calls negotiating a deal, trying to sound like someone who had closed one before. Across the table from people whose buy-in I had to win and whose real intentions I had to guess at. The hardest problems at a startup, it turned out, were rarely the technical ones. They were the people. What they want versus what they'll say out loud. When to push, when to give, how to get to a yes without handing away the things that mattered.

Nobody handed me a seat at that table. I negotiated for it, one sentence at a time, in a room where I was the easiest person in it to talk over. By the time I left I could stand in a room, read the people in it, and hold a point without my voice doing the thing it used to do.

Then the gap got much wider.

## Names without faces

Protocol Labs was the first place where the people around me had been writing code since before I was born. One of them had worked on Node.js itself. Another was an ex-PM from Google. The kind of résumés you read twice, once to make sure you read it right and once to actually believe it.

For months they were just handles in a Slack workspace to me, names attached to opinions so sharp I'd reread the messages trying to figure out how they thought. I was nineteen and aware of every year between us, and I went in braced for that silence to come back twice as loud.

It didn't. I'd ask a question and get a real answer, not a look. I'd push on something and someone would push back on the substance, never on my age. The gap I couldn't stop thinking about barely seemed to register on their side of the table.

Then I got invited to the Filecoin Dev Summit, in Bangkok, and the handles finally got faces.

I walked into a room full of people I'd only ever seen as grey avatars next to a username, and there they were. Voices. The way one of them held his coffee. The way two of them argued at a whiteboard and then went to lunch like nothing had happened. Putting a face to a name you've quietly respected for months does something strange to you. These weren't legends. They were people. Tired, funny, sometimes wrong, generous with what they knew.

Jet-lagged and the youngest person there by decades, in a room where some of them were in their fifties, I'd figured I would spend the week as a spectator. Instead I worked. I'd done the reading, my points held up, and by the second day I'd stopped keeping track of who was older than me. There wasn't room for it. There was the problem on the board, and whoever had something useful to say about it.

The best part of that whole trip wasn't the city, or the summit on paper. It was the work. Standing at a whiteboard with people whose code I'd studied, drawing up a solution to a problem none of us had cracked yet, finding the way out, figuring out how the thing would actually get built. I'd float an approach and watch someone twice my age take it seriously, push on it, lay something better on top. I don't think I'd had that much fun in a long time.

## Two weekends

It wasn't only at work that I was the youngest. Around that same stretch, still nineteen, I was spending my weekends in a different kind of room entirely.

Two of them, back to back. Unfold 2024 one weekend, EthIndia 2024 the next, both packed with people who had years on me. At one of them I looked up and there was a senior of mine from college, the gap between us wide enough to cover an entire degree, the two of us somehow in the same competition.

I won both.

Two weekends in a row, the youngest in the room. I'd be lying if I said that didn't change how I carried myself into the next one. You can be told you belong a hundred times and have it bounce off. It lands differently when the result says it for you.

## What happens when I talk now

I'm still the youngest engineer in the room. That part hasn't changed, and I've stopped waiting for it to.

What changed is the silence. It's gone.

I spend my days on calls with people from other companies, most of them twice my age, building the infrastructure that holds liquidity for an AMM DEX. The stakes are nothing like they were at nineteen. The money is real. The systems don't get a second draft. And I'm not the kid swallowing his point in the meeting anymore. I make the case. It lands more often than it doesn't. They hear me out, they push, I push back, and plenty of times I'm the one who turns out to be right.

What still catches me off guard is the reversal. These are people who were shipping software while I was learning to read, and there are moments now where the room turns to me and waits. Someone with twenty years on me will ask what I think, sit with the answer, and change their plan because of it. For so long the whole exercise was earning the right to speak. Somewhere along the way it quietly became the opposite, and I never caught the moment it flipped.

None of this arrived in a single moment. It got built across every one of those rooms. The startup where I learned to hold a point. The Slack threads. The summit. The two weekends. One conversation at a time, every time something I said survived contact with someone who knew more than I did.

## The chair

I used to think the goal was to close the gap. To get old enough, scarred enough, experienced enough that I'd finally belong.

I don't think that anymore. I belonged in all of those rooms the whole time. The work doesn't check your age before it decides whether it's any good, and the people worth working with don't either.

One day I won't be the youngest. Someone will walk in half the age of everyone there, certain they don't belong, sitting through that same silence, swallowing a point they're actually right about. When that happens, I want to be the one who turns to them and asks what they were about to say.

That's the part I'll keep. Not the wins, not the summit, not the title on the door. The fact that a room full of people who had every reason to talk over me chose to listen instead, and that it taught me to do the same.
